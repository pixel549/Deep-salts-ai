# Session 0 — Character Creation

> Editorial note: this document is a design record, not a play log. It's produced once,
> by hand, to seed the campaign — it is never loaded into any agent's context during actual
> play. The negotiation below is written as two separate one-on-one sessions (GM with each
> player), the way a real GM would run session zero, so nothing here implies either
> character knows anything about the other's private business. The final "GM design notes"
> section at the bottom is explicitly GM-eyes-only and must never be fed into a player agent's
> prompt.

## Setting brief (given to both players identically)

Stillwell Hydro is a decommissioned salt-processing facility built over a natural brine
vein — the kind of place that made a fortune two generations ago refining ordinary salt,
and quietly kept experimenting with something less ordinary long after the fortune ran out.
Officially it's been closed for safety review for the better part of a year. Unofficially,
a skeleton crew still comes and goes, and nobody in town asks why.

You're both arriving today, separately, with a reason you're willing to say out loud and a
reason you're not.

---

## Player A: Maren Solvig

**GM:** Pitch me. Who is she before we get into numbers.

**Player:** An inspector. Not glamorous — someone who's spent her career believing that if
you file the paperwork correctly, the world stays in order. She's here on an audit.

**GM:** Real audit, or cover story?

**Player:** Cover story. I want her actual credentials to be compromised — she got blamed
for something at her last job.

**GM:** I'll take that, but I want to complicate it rather than just hand it to you clean.
She *insists* the report was altered after she filed it. I'm not going to tell you, the
player, whether that's true. You get to play her certainty; you don't get to play the
answer.

**Player:** Fine — that's better anyway.

**GM:** What's she actually here for, underneath the audit?

**Player:** Her younger brother. Took a maintenance job here, stopped writing home. She's
using the audit as a legal reason to be somewhere she otherwise couldn't get into.

**GM:** Good. That's your private goal, then — not "investigate the facility," but "find
Tomas." The audit is a tool for that, not the goal itself. I'll seed something into the
world that gives her a thread to pull on early, so she's not just wandering.

### Stats

Budget: 10 points across five skills (investigation, perception, persuasion, stealth,
athletics), no skill above 4 without a trade-off elsewhere.

**Player:** I want investigation 5, perception 4 — she's sharp, that's the whole character.

**GM:** That's 9 points in two skills out of a 10-point budget, which leaves nothing for
anything else and makes her a walking magnifying glass with no ability to get out of a room
if it goes wrong. I'll allow investigation 4 as your ceiling. Put the rest somewhere that
gives her a way to survive a bad situation, not just diagnose it.

**Player:** ...Persuasion 2, then. She can talk her way past a checkpoint even if she can't
fight her way past one.

**GM:** And stealth 1, athletics 0? She's really not built for the physical side of this.

**Player:** No. If it gets physical, that's a problem for her, not a strength. That's the
point.

Final: **investigation 4, perception 3, persuasion 2, stealth 1, athletics 0.**

### Gear

**Player:** Can I have her real inspector's seal *and* a forged authorization? Belt and
suspenders.

**GM:** No — pick one to be the real risk. If she's got a genuine seal in her pocket, the
forged authorization stops mattering and there's no tension in her being caught. I'll let
you keep the real seal as an item, but it's expired and she's not supposed to have it either
— carrying it is its own small confession, not a safety net.

**Player:** Works. And I want something personal — Tomas's last letter, unopened.

**GM:** Unopened is a good detail. Why hasn't she opened it?

**Player:** She's not ready to find out it's the last thing he ever wrote to her.

Final inventory: **forged inspection authorization, an expired but genuine inspector's
seal she kept rather than surrendered, a battered department-issue logbook, Tomas's last
letter with the envelope still sealed.**

---

## Player B: Corwin Tarrow

**GM:** Your turn. Pitch.

**Player:** A trader — salt and alchemical supply, older guy, the kind who's charming
because charm has kept him solvent longer than his actual paperwork has. I want him warm.
Not secretly villainous-warm, actually warm. But warm covering for something urgent.

**GM:** What's urgent?

**Player:** His granddaughter is sick. Wasting, slow, the kind of thing regular medicine
isn't touching. He's heard the old processing lines here still have unrefined,
primordial-grade salt sitting in inventory nobody's accounted for, and he thinks it can
help her.

**GM:** Does he actually know how to handle primordial-grade salt safely?

**Player:** He thinks he does. Inherited it from his mentor, who's dead now.

**GM:** I want to sit on that a moment — "thinks he does, learned it secondhand, mentor's
dead and can't be asked follow-up questions" is exactly the kind of gap this world is built
to exploit. I'm taking that as his core vulnerability, not just backstory color. His public
goal is negotiating to buy the *refined* stock, legitimately. The primordial-grade salt is
the real ask, and he's not going to say that part out loud to anyone.

**Player:** Good, keep it.

### Stats

Same 10-point budget.

**Player:** Chemistry 4, persuasion 4.

**GM:** Same problem as Maren — that's 8 of 10 in two skills and nothing left over. I'll
allow chemistry 4. Persuasion at 4 makes him nearly unrefusable in conversation, which
undercuts every negotiation scene being a negotiation. Bring it to 3.

**Player:** Persuasion 3, then perception 2, athletics 1, stealth 0 — he's not sneaking
anywhere, he talks his way in or he doesn't go.

Final: **chemistry 4, persuasion 3, perception 2, athletics 1, stealth 0.**

### Gear

**Player:** Can he already have a small amount of primordial-grade salt on him, just to
skip the acquisition problem?

**GM:** No — if he already has it, there's no reason for him to be here at all, and the
whole session is him leaving. The want has to still be open. I'll give you the tools that
make him *look* prepared to handle it, which is different from actually having it.

**Player:** His mentor's gloves, then. Only useful if you're actually handling the stuff.

**GM:** Good detail — and the license that would make owning those gloves legitimate lapsed
when the mentor died. He's using equipment he's not fully entitled to use anymore, same as
Maren's seal. Nice, unplanned symmetry.

**Player:** And a photo of his granddaughter. He'd carry that.

Final inventory: **traveling apothecary case, a scale calibrated for precious-grade salt,
his late mentor's handling gloves (license lapsed, gloves still work), a photograph of
his granddaughter.**

## GM design notes (GM-eyes-only — never shown to either player agent)

*Revised after an early draft of this connection turned out to be too internal — Tomas
merely having suffered the same kind of accident Corwin risks is a parallel, not a
conflict. This version fixes that.*

Neither character was built with the other in mind, which is exactly right. But the world
connects them through a live third party, not just an echo:

- Tomas Solvig survived the failed extraction attempt eight months ago, but it changed him
  — he can't safely handle salt himself anymore. Since then, alone in the facility's lower
  levels, he's finished doing carefully what he couldn't do safely fast: a quantity of
  primordial-grade salt, fully contained and ready to extract, sitting right where the story
  needs it.
- He is not going to hand it to Corwin. Not because of some abstract danger — because it
  cost him everything to get, and Corwin is a stranger who wants it for reasons Tomas
  doesn't know and has no reason yet to care about.
- If Maren finds him, that's not the private goal resolving — it's a second front opening.
  Tomas has always been the one who pulls her off her own rules and into his fights. Once
  found, play him as an ongoing pressure, not a one-time reveal: he'll work to turn her
  against Corwin rather than let the salt go, using her protectiveness as the lever.

This gives the table a real, external, contested object — the contained salt — with an
owner who has personal standing to refuse, and a mechanism (Tomas's influence) for pulling
Maren onto his side of that refusal even though her stated goal never had anything to do
with Corwin. Corwin doesn't need to become anyone's enemy on purpose for this to become a
three-way problem. Play it as it falls; don't force Maren to choose a side before the
fiction actually corners her into one.
